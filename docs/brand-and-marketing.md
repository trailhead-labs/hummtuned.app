# Humm — Core Concepts, Values & Landing Page Marketing Plan

*Name: Humm (decided; clearance and domain in Part 5). Internal reference document, v2: fact-checked against the app.*

---

## Part 1 — What Humm Is

### One-line definition

Humm is a generative soundscape engine with research-grounded binaural programs that tune your state of mind: before coffee, on the bus, at your desk, in meditation, and before sleep.

### The product in one paragraph

Humm assembles a fresh ambient soundscape every session from a deep pool of natural textures, melodies, and effects, layered over binaural beat programs whose frequencies are traceable to published research. The user experience is deliberately minimal: pick a program, set the timer dial (or accept a sensible default), start the session. That's the whole interaction. Humm tunes you; you don't tune Humm.

### The programs

Seventeen programs, grouped by purpose in the app:

**Focus & Energy:** Focus & Concentration, Increased Attention, Learn & Memorize, Creativity, Conscious Thinking, Energize

**Calm & Meditation:** Relaxation, Deep Meditation, Intuition

**Sleep & Wake:** Lucid Dreaming, Power Nap, Restorative Sleep, Wake Up

**Experiential:** Astral Projection, Out-of-Body Experience, Deep Trance, Schumann Resonance

The purpose sections are for finding the right program; honesty about evidence is carried per program. The thirteen core programs are evidence-grounded: every frequency is traceable to published research and graded honestly in our synthesis, from Moderate (Deep Meditation, Restorative Sleep, Power Nap) down to Weak or traditional where that's the truth. The four experiential programs are rooted in tradition and subjective reports, and the app says so in plain words: offered for exploration, effects subjective, not scientifically established. Some core programs sit on traditional anchors too (no trial shows binaural beats induce lucid dreams, and we say that rather than hide it). Nothing is oversold as science.

### How a session works

1. **Pick a program.** A short sheet tells you what it does, how to use it, and how long it wants.
2. **Set the timer dial.** Sensible defaults per program; it remembers your own length per program, so this step is optional.
3. **Start.** Headphones on. That's it.

Everything else is tucked away until you go looking: session pacing (start hold, buildup, ramp down, end hold, each seeded with grounded per-program defaults), a sound panel (levels, which sounds join the mix, how often the mix shifts), and a per-program end chime. Nothing on the surface asks the user to make a decision they aren't qualified or inclined to make.

## Part 2 — Core Concepts

### 1. The engine, not the playlist

Every session is mixed fresh from a deep sample pool, and the mix keeps shifting as it plays. There is no track to memorize and no soundscape that wears out from familiarity. This is the technical moat: repetition is what makes ambient audio fade into annoyance, and Humm is built to keep it from setting in.

### 2. Simplicity is a feature, not a compromise

The absence of surface controls is the product working as intended. Competing apps expose frequency pickers and preset builders, implicitly transferring responsibility for efficacy onto the user. Humm takes the opposite position: the defaults are the research. Tuning exists for those who go looking, but hiding it is the science-grounded choice.

### 3. The app tunes you, not the other way around

Binaural entrainment is something that happens *to* your awareness, automatically, while you do something else (or nothing at all). The interaction model mirrors the mechanism: tap, go, let it work.

### 4. A state engine for the whole day

Humm is not a sleep app or a focus app. It spans the full arc of consciousness: waking up, powering through, napping, learning, drifting off, and exploring the edges. It's used before the first coffee and after the last light goes out.

### 5. The shift, then the hold

Every session starts at a threshold: asleep to awake, restless to calm, scattered to focused, awake to dreaming. Humm makes the shift, then holds the state for as long as you're in it. You reach for it *before* (work, sleep, the day) and leave it playing *during*: through the deep work, the meditation, the night.

### 6. A better reflex

The phone is already the first thing you reach for in the morning and the last thing at night. Humm gives that reflex somewhere better to land: instead of doomscrolling toward the day, a soothing soundscape while entrainment nudges you toward alertness on the way to the coffee machine. Same gesture, opposite effect. Humm slots into the routines you already have and quietly replaces what the feed was doing to them.

### 7. Honest science, honest wonder

Evidence is graded per program and labeled in plain words, in the app and on the page. The core programs cite their grounding. The experiential programs invite exploration without pretending to a certainty that doesn't exist. This honesty is a differentiator in a category full of overclaiming.

## Part 3 — Core Values

**Respect for attention.** No ads. Ever. An app whose entire purpose is protecting and directing your attention cannot ethically sell that attention to someone else.

**Respect for privacy.** No tracking. Sessions are yours. What state you're in, when you sleep, what you're working toward: none of it is data to be harvested. There is nothing to harvest.

**Respect for the wallet.** One purchase, per device, for life. No subscription treadmill, no feature drip, no paywall creep. Try everything free for a week; if it's for you, buy it once and own it.

**Respect for the user's intelligence.** Honest labeling of what's research and what's exploration. No mystical overclaiming, no clinical cosplay.

**Respect for the moment.** The app gets out of the way. No streaks, no gamification, no notifications begging for engagement. Humm succeeds when you forget it's running.

## Part 4 — Landing Page Marketing Plan

### Positioning statement

*For people who want to direct their state of mind without fiddling with an app, Humm is a generative binaural soundscape engine that tunes you, with research-grounded defaults, a one-tap session, and a soundscape mixed fresh every time. Unlike subscription soundscape apps, Humm has no ads, no tracking, and a one-time lifetime price.*

### Audience (two overlapping readers)

1. **The pragmatist** wants focus, sleep, and naps that work; skeptical of woo; responds to research citations, restraint, and the anti-subscription stance.
2. **The explorer** is curious about lucid dreaming, Schumann resonance, out-of-body practice; responds to depth, honesty, and being treated as an adult rather than a customer segment.

The page must never force a choice between them. Lead with the pragmatist (broader market, builds trust), reveal the explorer content deeper down the page.

### Tone

Calm, precise, quietly confident. Short sentences. No exclamation marks. No wellness clichés ("unlock," "journey," "transform"). The copy should feel like the product: minimal surface, depth underneath.

### Page structure (top to bottom)

**1. Hero.**
- Headline: the state-change promise in plainest form. Candidate directions:
  - "Tune your mind. One tap."
  - "Press play on the state you need."
  - "Focus, sleep, wake, dream. One dial."
- Subline: "Binaural programs grounded in published research, inside a soundscape mixed fresh every session."
- Primary CTA: **Try free for a week** (single button; no competing CTAs).
- Visual: the actual UI, the timer dial and program selector. The interface is minimal enough to *be* the hero image. Show, don't describe.

**2. The three-step ritual.**
Pick. Set. Start. Three frames, almost no copy. This section proves the simplicity claim in under five seconds of scanning.

**3. The engine (differentiator #1).**
"Every session is mixed fresh. Familiarity never sets in." Explain in two or three sentences why repetition kills ambient audio and how the generative mix avoids it. This is the claim no playlist-based competitor can copy.

**4. The programs.**
Seventeen programs in the app's four purpose groups: Focus & Energy, Calm & Meditation, Sleep & Wake, and a visually distinct *Experiential* section. Each program gets one line on what it's for and when to use it. Carry the honest evidence labeling onto the page; it builds more trust than hiding the experiential programs would.

**5. A day with Humm (use-case strip).**
Five moments, written with the name as the verb: Humm before your first coffee · Humm on the bus · Humm through the deep work · Humm in meditation · Humm before sleep. This section repositions Humm from "sleep app" to "all-day state companion," and each moment is a habit swap: these are the exact minutes that otherwise go to the feed. The morning line can say it outright: don't doomscroll toward the day; Humm your way to the coffee machine.

**6. The science, briefly.**
Two or three sentences on binaural beats and entrainment, with links to the underlying research (a dedicated /science page is worth building; it's a trust asset and an SEO asset). State plainly: the defaults are set from the research, which is why nothing on the surface asks you to pick a frequency.

**7. What Humm doesn't do (differentiator #2).**
This section carries the values and doubles as the pricing rationale:
- No ads.
- No tracking.
- No subscription.
- No account required.
- No gamification, streaks, or notifications.
- Works entirely on your device, offline included.

Frame it as philosophy, not feature list: *"An app for your attention shouldn't be designed to consume it."*

**8. Pricing.**
- **Free for a week.** Full access, every program, no card required. (If no card is truly required, say so explicitly; it lifts trial conversion.)
- **Then one price, once, per device.** Lifetime. All future programs included (if true; decide and commit, because "all future updates included" is a strong lifetime-purchase amplifier).
- Anchor against the category: "Most soundscape apps cost $60–70 *per year*, forever." Let the reader do the math.
- Be transparent that the license is per device. Clarity here prevents refund friction and bad reviews later.

**9. FAQ.**
Cover, at minimum:
- Do I need headphones? (Yes: binaural beats need each ear to hear its own tone, so stereo headphones are required. Say this early and plainly; it's also a credibility signal because informed users know it.)
- What does "research-grounded" mean here, exactly?
- What's the difference between the core and experiential programs?
- Why no subscription?
- Why per device?
- Does it work offline? (Yes. Everything runs on the device; this belongs in section 7 too as a privacy-story amplifier.)
- Is this a medical device? (No. Include a short, clear disclaimer: Humm supports focus, rest, and relaxation; it does not diagnose or treat any condition. Keep all page copy consistent with this.)

**10. Closing CTA.**
Repeat the single CTA: **Try free for a week.** One line above it that echoes the hero. Nothing else.

### Copy rules

- **Humm is a verb.** "Humm before coffee. Humm on the bus. Humm before sleep." Very few apps get to conjugate themselves; make it the copy signature wherever a use-case is named. (Naming is parked in Part 5; the verb stays Humm in copy whichever mark wins.)
- One CTA verb across the entire page ("Try free for a week"). Never "Download," "Get started," and "Try" competing.
- Every claim about the core programs must be traceable to the /science page. Never let marketing copy outrun the citations.
- Never use "cure," "treat," "therapy," or clinical-outcome language. "Supports," "designed for," "grounded in research on…" are the ceiling.
- The experiential programs are described with curiosity, not certainty: "rooted in decades of subjective reports and contemplative tradition."
- Word count target: the entire page should read in under three minutes. If the product is simple, the page must be too.

### Trust signals worth adding when available

Research citations on the /science page (the synthesis behind the app draws on 28 sources; use them); a short "how the engine works" note (technical honesty attracts the pragmatist); App Store rating once live; a one-paragraph founder note on why no ads, no tracking, no subscription. A personal stance is more credible than a policy page.

### What to measure (without tracking users)

The no-tracking promise applies to user behavior in the app. For the landing page, either use a privacy-respecting, cookieless analytics tool (aggregate page counts only) and say so in the footer, or measure nothing and let trial-to-purchase conversion be the only metric. Whichever you choose, the footer should state it in one sentence. Consistency between the promise and the page is itself marketing.

## Part 5 — Naming (Pending Decision)

### Status

**Humm is a go as the app name** (decided July 2026), pending the clearance checks below. The open question has moved from the name to the domain; a shortlist is chosen (see Domain).

The stakes stay low either way, because the verb is the asset and the mark is just the container: "Humm before coffee" works as copy whatever the registered brand ends up being. If clearance forces a retreat from bare Humm, the runner-up containers from the exploration (Ohumm, then Humming) remain viable with the verb unchanged. What clearance decides is the container, not the voice.

### Why the Humm family won

Every alternative lane was tested in the naming exploration: synonyms (Attune, Align, Cohere), mechanism names (Phase, Dyad), engine names (Alea), state names (Poise, Cusp, Liminal). Each failed at least one filter that Humm passes all of:

- **Onomatopoeia.** The name is the product's sound. No explanation needed.
- **Simplicity.** One syllable. Matches the product philosophy (simplicity is a feature).
- **The verb test.** Habit products get verbed in daily speech. "I hummed for ten minutes before coffee" is natural English, with a bonus double meaning: used the app, or was contentedly humming. Almost no alternative name verbs at all.
- **Habit positioning.** The counter-doomscroll ritual ("Humm before coffee. Humm on the bus. Humm before sleep.") conjugates the brand into the marketing copy. This verb-first pattern is the copy signature (see Copy rules) and survives whichever candidate wins.

### The decision, and the fallback ladder

**Humm won.** The purest form: strongest verb, strongest sound-identity, shortest. Its weaknesses are external (the word is crowded, see clearance status, and the bare .com/.app are taken), which is acceptable because the brand carries the weight, not the URL.

If clearance kills bare Humm, the ladder is: **Ohumm** (om and humm fused; adds chant-resonance, materially more distinctive, easier registration, ohumm.app open; cost: slightly less pure, verb becomes indirect), then **Humming** (the verb form as the brand; evokes an engine running well, likely clears more easily; cost: two syllables, less iconic as a mark). Either keeps Humm as the verb in all copy.

### Domain

Bare humm.com/.app and humm.audio are taken. The domain search is treated as a branding exercise: every candidate must be grounded in the product philosophy, and the container logic applies here too (the domain needn't carry the verb; copy does that; the container should be short, clean, and ownable).

**Rejected, with reasons:**
- **thinkhumm.app.** Free, and close to the dormant neurotech company's identity (thinkhumm.com, now offline). Rejected on two grounds: adjacency invites confusion and hands any successor entity a bad-faith narrative in a domain dispute, and "think" is the wrong verb for a product whose interaction model is *stop thinking, tap, let it work*.
- **tryhumm.app.** CTA-aligned but didn't land. Dropped on taste.
- **dailyhumm.app.** Fails the values filter: "daily" whispers streaks and obligation, and the product promises neither.
- **Any "tune humm" ordering** (tunehumm, tuneyourhumm, tunetohumm). Fails the agency filter: the app tunes you, you don't tune the app. Domains must keep Humm as the agent.

**The tune family (chosen lane).** Tuning is the mechanism in plain English (entrainment is tuning-to), and conjugating it around Humm with the agency pointing the right way yields a coherent grammar rather than a single domain:
- **hummtuned.app**: primary, registered July 2026. Shortest, cleanest to say, and it rides the fine-tuned idiom (precision-instrument connotations that suit the research-grounded positioning).
- **tunedbyhumm.app**: second. Explicit agent direction, with a badge quality ("tuned by Humm" works as a share line, subtitle, or footer signature).
- **"Humm tunes you"**: the tagline form, regardless of which domain wins.

End-state: hummtuned.app as the domain, "Humm tunes you" as the tagline, "tuned by Humm" as the badge. Three conjugations of one idea, all keeping Humm as the agent.

**Fallback lane:** **justhumm.app**, from the simplicity value ("just humm" is the product philosophy in two words). Held in reserve so a registrar failure in the tune lane switches lanes at full strength instead of descending it.

**Registrar status:** **hummtuned.app is registered** (July 2026); it is the domain. Still to take: tunedbyhumm.app (badge line, redirect to primary) and the single-m typo variants (humtuned.app and kin) as redirects; "hum" is the English word, so fingers will drop the second m.

### Name clearance status (as of July 2026; informal check, not legal advice)

- **The old blocker is dead.** The neurotech company "Human Universal Mind Machines" (thinkhumm) filed US application 88774655 for HUMM in 2020, covering stimulation patches and mobile apps for alertness, sleep, and calm. It was allowed, but they never filed a Statement of Use; the record stops at the second extension (August 2021), so the application went abandoned around 2022 without ever registering. The company itself went silent after its beta.
- **A new wrinkle is live.** A "Humm Inc." filed fresh US application 99116157 on April 2, 2025 for "non-invasive medical devices for use in treating stress and promoting relaxation," plausibly the same team repositioned. Action: look up serial 99116157 on USPTO TSDR and check whether the goods include Class 9 (software). Device-only (Class 10): coexistence with a soundscape app is arguable. Class 9 included: bare Humm becomes risky and Ohumm/Humming rise.
- **Background noise, manageable.** The Australian fintech "humm group" (buy-now-pay-later; different classes, but it dominates search results and the App Store for the bare word), phonetically identical "HUM" wellness brands (HUM Nutrition), and a Polish pro-audio maker (hum-audio).
- **Still to do.** EUIPO check (the company is NL-based; the US register alone is insufficient), verify the fintech's EU registrations, confirm ohumm.app and the other candidate domains at a registrar (Porkbun or Namecheap), and, before any spend on design or filings, a flat-fee attorney knockout search. US common-law rights from thinkhumm's 2020–2022 product sales are a wrinkle only an attorney can weigh.
